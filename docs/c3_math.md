## 1. Plant Model

### 1.1 State and Control Dimensions

The Drake `MultibodyPlant` has two rigid bodies beyond the world: the 7-DOF Franka
Panda arm and one unactuated floating-base object (box or sphere).

| Symbol | Value | Meaning |
|--------|-------|---------|
| $n_{q,\text{arm}}$ | 7 | arm revolute joint positions |
| $n_{q,\text{obj}}$ | 7 | object floating-base positions: $(q_w,q_x,q_y,q_z,x,y,z)$ |
| $n_q$ | **14** | total generalized positions |
| $n_{v,\text{arm}}$ | 7 | arm joint velocities |
| $n_{v,\text{obj}}$ | 6 | object floating-base velocities: $(\omega_x,\omega_y,\omega_z,v_x,v_y,v_z)$ |
| $n_v$ | **13** | total generalized velocities |

### 1.3 The Kinematic Map $\dot{q} = N(q)\,v$

For floating-body systems the quaternion rate $\dot{q}_w, \dot{q}_x, \dot{q}_y, \dot{q}_z$ is a
non-trivial linear map of the body angular velocity. Drake handles this via
`MapVelocityToQDot`. The matrix $N(q) \in \mathbb{R}^{14 \times 13}$ is built
column-by-column by evaluating `MapVelocityToQDot` on each basis vector
$e_i \in \mathbb{R}^{13}$:

$$
N(q)_{:,\,i} = \texttt{MapVelocityToQDot}(q,\, e_i), \quad i = 1,\ldots,13
\tag{1.3}
$$

(`control/lcs_formulator.py:252-257`)

For the 7 revolute arm joints $\dot{q} = v$ (identity sub-block); only the
object's quaternion rows are non-trivial.

## 2. Contact Geometry

### 2.1 Signed Distance and Witness Points

At each control step, Drake's SceneGraph evaluates pairwise signed distance for all
geometry pairs within a threshold of $0.10\,\text{m}$:

$$
\phi_i = \texttt{ComputeSignedDistancePairwiseClosestPoints}(\varepsilon = 0.10)
\tag{2.1}
$$

(`control/lcs_formulator.py:113-115`)

Only pairs between the EE geometry (`pusher`) and the object geometry are kept;
all arm self-contacts, arm-table pairs, and object-floor pairs are discarded
(`control/lcs_formulator.py:120-127`). This prevents $n_c = 32$–59 phantom contacts from
corrupting the QP.

Each surviving pair yields:
- $\phi_i \in \mathbb{R}$: signed gap distance (negative = penetrating)
- $\hat{n}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$: contact normal `nhat_BA_W` (unit, from body B toward body A)
- $p^A_{C_a}$, $p^B_{C_b}$: witness points on each body

**Concrete value** at `step=50` (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:887`):
$n_c^{\text{normals}} = 1$, $\phi_0 = 0.01928\,\text{m}$.

---

### 2.2 Translational Velocity Jacobians

For each contact pair, Drake computes translational Jacobians at the witness
points (`control/lcs_formulator.py:151-158`):

$$
J_A \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 13}, \quad
J_B \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 13}
\tag{2.2}
$$

via `CalcJacobianTranslationalVelocity(context, kV, body_frame, witness_pt, W, W)`.

The **relative contact velocity Jacobian** (`control/lcs_formulator.py:160`):

$$
J_{\text{rel}} = J_A - J_B \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 13}
\tag{2.3}
$$

### 2.3 Normal Jacobian Row

The normal Jacobian row for contact $i$ is (`control/lcs_formulator.py:183`):

$$
J_n[i,\,:] = \hat{n}_i^\top \, J_{\text{rel}} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 13}
\tag{2.4}

Drake assigns body A/B arbitrarily.  `nhat_BA_W` points from B toward A.

### 2.4 Polyhedral Tangent Basis

A 4-edge polyhedral pyramid is used per contact (`control/lcs_formulator.py:186-194`):

$$
\hat{t}_1 = \frac{\hat{n}_i \times \hat{r}}{\|\hat{n}_i \times \hat{r}\|}, \quad
\hat{t}_2 = \hat{n}_i \times \hat{t}_1
\tag{2.5}
$$

where $\hat{r}$ is a deterministic reference axis (`control/lcs_formulator.py:186-188`):
$$
\hat{r} = [1, 0, 0] \quad \text{unless } |\hat{n}_i \cdot [1,0,0]| > 0.99,
\text{ in which case } \hat{r} = [0,1,0]
$$

The four tangent Jacobian rows are (`control/lcs_formulator.py:193-194`):

$$
J_t[4i:4i+4,\,:] =
\begin{bmatrix}
\hat{t}_1^\top J_{\text{rel}} \\
-\hat{t}_1^\top J_{\text{rel}} \\
\hat{t}_2^\top J_{\text{rel}} \\
-\hat{t}_2^\top J_{\text{rel}}
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 13}
\tag{2.6}
$$

The four **polyhedral** components $\lambda_t = [\lambda_t^{(0)}, \lambda_t^{(1)}, \lambda_t^{(2)}, \lambda_t^{(3)}]$ are all $\geq 0$.
They encode the Cartesian tangent force (`control/admm_solver.py:109-113`):

$$
F_t = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_t^{(0)} - \lambda_t^{(1)} \\ \lambda_t^{(2)} - \lambda_t^{(3)} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^2
\tag{2.7}
$$

The friction cone constraint (enforced by the $\delta$-projection) is the **Lorentz cone** on $(F_t, \lambda_n)$:

$$
\|F_t\|_2 \leq \mu\,\lambda_n
\tag{2.8}
$$

(NOT a linear polyhedral constraint; see Section 7 for the projection.)

### 3.4 Why $\|D\| \gg \|B\|$: The Dominant Coupling

$$
\frac{\|D\|_F}{\|B\|_F} = \frac{21.96}{0.41} \approx 53.6
\tag{3.4}
$$

$D$ encodes $\Delta t\, M^{-1} J_c^\top$. The object is light ($m = 0.2\,\text{kg}$) so
$M^{-1}$ has large entries for the object rows. Even a small contact force
$\lambda_n \sim 1\,\text{N}$ produces a large acceleration of the box.
By contrast $B$ maps joint torques through the arm's inertia ($\sim 1$–$10\,\text{kg\,m}^2$)
which suppresses its norm. This means the contact channel is 54× more efficient per
unit of "force budget" than the torque channel — the QP primarily uses $\lambda$ to
move the box and $u$ to reposition the EE.

When $D$ collapses to $(27, 0)$ (no contact within 0.10 m), the box becomes
dynamically decoupled from the QP cost and the arm freezes unless the EE-approach
cost (Section 5.2) provides an alternative gradient.

## 4. Complementarity Conditions

### 4.1 Ideal (Hard) Complementarity

The theoretically correct contact model requires (for each contact $i$):

$$
0 \leq \phi_i \perp \lambda_{n,i} \geq 0
\tag{4.1}
$$

meaning either the gap is open ($\phi_i > 0$) and no force is applied ($\lambda_{n,i} = 0$),
or the bodies are in contact ($\phi_i = 0$) and the normal force is non-negative.
This is a **complementarity constraint** and makes the problem a Linear Complementarity
Problem (LCP), which is NP-hard to solve globally.

Additionally, friction-cone complementarity in the full LCP form introduces a slack
variable $\gamma_i \geq 0$ encoding the amount of slip:

$$
\mu\,\lambda_{n,i} - \sum_j \lambda_{t,i,j} \geq 0, \quad \gamma_i \geq 0,
\quad \left(\mu\,\lambda_{n,i} - \sum_j \lambda_{t,i,j}\right)\gamma_i = 0
\tag{4.2}
$$

The C3 formulation **does not enforce Eq. (4.1) or (4.2) as hard constraints**
inside the QP.

---

### 4.2 Soft Complementarity (What the Solver Does)

Instead, a linear penalty is added to the QP objective (`control/admm_solver.py:285-290`):

$$
w_{\text{comp}} \sum_{i=1}^{n_{\text{normals}}} \lambda_{n,i} \cdot \max(\phi_i,\, 0)
\tag{4.3}
$$

with $w_{\text{comp}} = 100$ (`control/admm_solver.py:72`). When $\phi_i > 0$ (gap open) this
adds a linear cost per unit of planned normal force, biasing the solution toward
$\lambda_{n,i} = 0$ until actual contact is achieved.

This term enters the linear cost vector $q$ in the QP (`control/admm_solver.py:288-290`):
```python
q_ref[li : li + num_normals] += w_comp * phi_gap
```
where `phi_gap = clip(phi, 0, None)`.

**Observed value at step 50** (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:898`):
`Soft complementarity: w_comp=100.0000 · Σ λ_n·max(φ,0) = 0.0270`

The Lorentz-cone constraint (2.8) is similarly **not** a QP constraint — it is
enforced by the $\delta$-projection step in ADMM (Section 7.2).

### 5.2 EE-Approach Cost — $J_p^\top J_p$ Augmentation

When no contact exists ($D \approx 0$), the QP has no path to move the box, and
the arm freezes (minimises $u^\top R u \to 0$). The EE-approach term adds a
Jacobian-linearised cost driving the arm toward a **proxy contact point** behind
the object (`control/task_costs.py:218-437`).

**Proxy definition** (`control/task_costs.py:226-231`):

$$
p_{\text{proxy}} = \begin{bmatrix} x_{\text{obj}} - d_{\text{push}}\,\hat{g}_x \\ y_{\text{obj}} - d_{\text{push}}\,\hat{g}_y \\ z_{\text{ref}} \end{bmatrix},
\quad \hat{g} = \frac{p_{\text{goal}} - p_{\text{obj}}}{\|p_{\text{goal}} - p_{\text{obj}}\|}
\tag{5.2}
$$

with $d_{\text{push}} = 0.05\,\text{m}$ (from `tasks.yaml`). The minus sign places the proxy
on the **opposite** side of the object from the goal — behind the box to be pushed.

**Three-stage approach** blends the effective proxy target depending on
$\|e_{\text{EE-box}}\|$ (`control/task_costs.py:257-267`):
- Stage 1 ($> 0.25\,\text{m}$): target pre-approach point at $0.18\,\text{m}$ behind box
- Stage 2 ($0.10$–$0.25\,\text{m}$): blend pre-approach $\to$ approach waypoint ($0.20\,\text{m}$ behind)
- Stage 3 ($< 0.10\,\text{m}$): blend approach waypoint $\to$ contact proxy

**Jacobian-based linearisation** (`control/task_costs.py:415-437`):

The EE translational Jacobian $J_{\text{EE}} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 13}$ is evaluated at
the current state. The arm sub-block is:

$$
J_{\text{arm}} = J_{\text{EE}}[:,\;0:7] \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 7}
\tag{5.3}
$$

(`control/task_costs.py:421`)

The $Q$ augmentation for EE approach (`control/task_costs.py:434`):

$$
Q[0:7,\;0:7] \mathrel{+}= 2\,w_{\text{ee}}\,J_{\text{arm}}^\top J_{\text{arm}}
\tag{5.4}
$$

The reference arm joint positions are shifted by the damped pseudoinverse
(`control/task_costs.py:427-437`):

$$
\delta q_{\text{arm}} = J_{\text{arm}}^\top \left(J_{\text{arm}} J_{\text{arm}}^\top + \lambda I_3\right)^{-1} e_{\text{EE}}, \quad \lambda = 0.001
\tag{5.5}
$$

$$
x_{\text{ref}}[0:7] = q_{\text{arm}} + \delta q_{\text{arm}}
\tag{5.6}
$$

This makes the EE-approach cost a quadratic in arm positions, consistent with the
LQR structure.

**Effect on $Q$**: The resulting $Q[0:7,0:7]$ block is a dense symmetric PSD matrix
(42 off-diagonal nonzero entries in the full $27 \times 27$ $Q$). From the first-step
`[MATH.Q]` (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:57`):

\tag{5.7}

### 6.2 The QP Problem

OSQP minimises (`control/admm_solver.py:9-10`):

$$
\min_z \quad \tfrac{1}{2}\, z^\top P_{\text{sym}}\, z + q^\top z
\tag{6.4}
$$

subject to (`control/admm_solver.py:303-342`):

$$
C_{\text{eq}}\, z = b_{\text{eq}} \quad (567\text{ rows})
\tag{6.5a}
$$

$$
\lambda_{n,i} \geq 0 \quad \forall i \in \{0,\ldots,N-1\} \quad \text{(BoundingBox)}
\tag{6.5b}
$$

$$
-30 \leq u_{i,j} \leq 30\,\text{Nm} \quad \forall i,j \quad \text{(BoundingBox)}
\tag{6.5c}
$$

The 567 equality rows are (`control/admm_solver.py:299-318`):
- $n_x = 27$ rows: $x_0 = x_{\text{current}}$ (initial state fixation)
- $N \times n_x = 20 \times 27 = 540$ rows: LCS dynamics for steps $0,\ldots,N-1$:
  $A\,x_i + D\,\lambda_i + B\,u_i - x_{i+1} = -d$

$(N+1)\,n_x = 21 \times 27 = 567$ total. (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:893`)

---

### 6.3 What Is in $P$ and $q$

**$P$ matrix** (`control/admm_solver.py:262-294`):

$$
P = \underbrace{2\,\text{blkdiag}(Q,Q,\ldots,Q_N)}_{\text{(a) tracking cost}} +
    \underbrace{R\text{ blocks}}_{\text{(a) control cost}} +
    \underbrace{\rho\, I_{807}}_{\text{(b) ADMM augment}}
\tag{6.6}
$$

Then $P_{\text{sym}} = \frac{1}{2}(P + P^\top) + 10^{-8} I$ (OSQP symmetry requirement, `control/admm_solver.py:294`).

**$q$ vector** has three contributions:

**(a) Nominal MPC linear term** (`control/admm_solver.py:269,273`):
$$
q_{\text{ref}}[x_i\text{-block}] = -2\,Q\,x_{\text{ref}}, \quad
q_{\text{ref}}[x_N\text{-block}] = -2\,Q_N\,x_{\text{ref}}
\tag{6.7}
$$

**(b) ADMM coupling** (`control/admm_solver.py:365-367`):
$$
q_{\text{total}} = q_{\text{ref}} - \rho\,(\delta - \omega)
\tag{6.8}
$$
Expanding: the ADMM augmented cost is $(\rho/2)\,\|z - \delta + \omega\|^2$, whose
linear part w.r.t. $z$ is $-\rho(\delta - \omega)$.

**(c) Soft complementarity** (`control/admm_solver.py:285-290`):
$$
q[\lambda_{n,i}\text{-block}] \mathrel{+}= w_{\text{comp}} \cdot \max(\phi_i,\, 0)
\tag{6.9}
$$

---

### 6.4 What Is NOT in the QP

| Constraint | How it is handled |
|---|---|
| Friction cone $\|F_t\|_2 \leq \mu\,\lambda_n$ | $\delta$-projection in ADMM (Section 7.2) |
| Complementarity $\phi_i \lambda_{n,i} = 0$ | Soft penalty only (Eq. 4.3) |
| $\lambda_t \geq 0$ (polyhedral components) | Implicit via split-back in projection |

The absence of the second-order cone constraint (6.8) is what makes the
problem a **QP solvable by OSQP** rather than an SOCP (which OSQP cannot solve)
or an MICP.

**QP properties at step=50** (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:895`):
`P shape=(807,807), symmetric=True, pos-semidef=True, cond(P)=1.0000e+05`

## 7. ADMM Consensus

### 7.1 Two-Block Decomposition

The ADMM loop enforces consensus between:
1. **$z$-update** (QP, dynamics-feasible): finds $z$ minimising the augmented
   Lagrangian while satisfying LCS dynamics and torque bounds.
2. **$\delta$-update** (Lorentz cone projection, cone-feasible): projects
   $z[\lambda] + \omega[\lambda]$ onto the friction cone for each contact/step.
3. **$\omega$-update** (dual ascent): accumulates the consensus violation.

Three iterations are run per control step (configured in `main.py`).

---

### 7.2 $\delta$-Update: Lorentz Cone Projection

For each horizon step $i$ and each contact, the per-contact projection is
(`control/admm_solver.py:376-384`):

$$
\delta[\lambda_i] = \Pi_C\!\left(z[\lambda_i] + \omega[\lambda_i]\right)
\tag{7.1}
$$

where $\Pi_C$ is the Lorentz cone projection (`control/admm_solver.py:83-152`).

The projection converts polyhedral $\lambda_t \to F_t$ (Eq. 2.7), applies the cone
in 2D Cartesian space, then converts back. Three cases (`control/admm_solver.py:123-131`):

**Case 1** — inside cone ($\|F_t\|_2 \leq \mu\,\lambda_n$):

$$
\delta_n = \lambda_n, \quad F_{t,\text{new}} = F_t
\tag{7.2}
$$

(`control/admm_solver.py:123-125`)

**Case 2** — polar cone ($\mu\,\|F_t\|_2 \leq -\lambda_n$):

$$
\delta_n = 0, \quad F_{t,\text{new}} = 0
\tag{7.3}
$$

(`control/admm_solver.py:126-128`)

**Case 3** — surface projection (friction-saturated):

$$
s = \frac{\lambda_n + \mu\,\|F_t\|_2}{1 + \mu^2}, \quad
\delta_n = s, \quad
F_{t,\text{new}} = \frac{\mu\,s}{\|F_t\|_2}\,F_t
\tag{7.4}
$$

(`control/admm_solver.py:129-131`)

The polyhedral components are recovered by `max(·, 0)` splitting (`control/admm_solver.py:134-140`):
$\lambda_t^{(0)} = \max(F_{t,\text{new}}[0], 0)$, $\lambda_t^{(1)} = \max(-F_{t,\text{new}}[0], 0)$, etc.

---

### 7.3 $\omega$-Update

(`control/admm_solver.py:386`)

$$
\omega \leftarrow \omega + z - \delta
\tag{7.5}
$$

The $x$ and $u$ blocks are unconstrained: their $\delta$ equals $z + \omega$ (pass-through),
so their $\omega$-accumulation vanishes. Only the $\lambda$ blocks drive residuals.

---

### 7.4 Concrete Example — Step 50

From `[MATH.δ]` at step=50 (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:900-906`):

| Horizon steps in Case 1 | Case 2 | Case 3 |
|------------------------|--------|--------|
| 0 | 0 | **20** |

All 20 horizon steps are in Case 3. For the first contact at step $t=0$:

| Quantity | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Pre-projection $\lambda_n$ (from QP) | $0.0140$ |
| Pre-projection $\|\lambda_t\|$ (polyhedral) | $0.4516$ |
| $\mu \cdot \lambda_n$ (cone bound) | $0.0056$ |
| Post-projection $\delta_n$ | $0.2295$ |
| Post-projection $\|\delta_t\|$ | $0.0918$ |

**Physical interpretation**: The QP solution requests a large tangential force
($\|F_t\| \approx 0.63\,\text{N}$ Cartesian) but provides almost no normal force
($\lambda_n = 0.014\,\text{N}$), which allows only $\mu \lambda_n = 0.006\,\text{N}$ of friction.
The Case 3 projection boosts $\delta_n$ from $0.014 \to 0.229$ (a $16\times$ increase)
to make the contact kinematically consistent — the solver is saying "to achieve the
tangential force you want, the normal force must be at least $0.23\,\text{N}$."
This is the ADMM's mechanism for encoding the Coulomb friction constraint without
adding a second-order cone to the QP.

---

### 7.5 Residuals and Convergence

Primal residual (contact variables only) (`control/admm_solver.py:402`):

$$
r_{\text{primal}} = \left\| \lambda_{\text{vec}} - \delta_{\text{vec}} \right\|_2
\tag{7.6}
$$

Dual residual (`control/admm_solver.py:403`):

$$
r_{\text{dual}} = \rho\, \left\| \delta_{\text{vec}} - \delta_{\text{prev}} \right\|_2
\tag{7.7}
$$

Early termination when $r_{\text{primal}} < 10^{-3}$ and $r_{\text{dual}} < 10^{-3}$
(`control/admm_solver.py:422-425`).

**At step=50** (`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:890`):
`primal: 0.1091→0.4718  dual: 5.7286→9.3890  iters=3/3`
(not converged — 3 iterations is insufficient for tight complementarity).

---

### 7.6 Adaptive $\rho$ (Boyd §3.4.1)

Every 10 iterations (`control/admm_solver.py:408`):
- If $r_{\text{primal}} > 10\,r_{\text{dual}}$: $\rho \leftarrow 2\rho$, $\omega \leftarrow \omega/2$
- If $r_{\text{dual}} > 10\,r_{\text{primal}}$: $\rho \leftarrow \rho/2$, $\omega \leftarrow 2\omega$

(`control/admm_solver.py:409-420`)

**This logic never triggers in production**: `max_iter = 3 < 10` (the trigger threshold),
so the check at `(it+1) % 10 == 0` is never reached.
(`results/baseline_after_meeting.txt:910`:
`Note: adaptive-ρ fires every 10 iters; current max_iter=3 ← never triggers!`)

The ratio at step=50 would call for halving $\rho$ (ratio = 0.0502 < 0.1), but it
is blocked by the iteration limit.

## 8. Receding Horizon Loop

Per-step pseudocode, mirroring `control/ci_mpc_c3.py:53-256`:

```

                                                             # control/ci_mpc_c3.py:80-81

                                                             # control/ci_mpc_c3.py:143-145
```

The planning horizon is $N = 20$ steps at $\Delta t = 0.05\,\text{s}$, giving a
$1.0\,\text{s}$ lookahead. The control rate is $\Delta t_{\text{ctrl}} = 0.01\,\text{s}$
(the Drake simulator step), so only $u_{\text{seq}}[0]$ is applied before the next
re-linearisation.

## 9. Failure Modes and Verification

### 9.1 Contact-Distance Threshold Gates Everything

`extract_lcs_contacts` only finds contact pairs within `distance_threshold = 0.10 m`
(`control/lcs_formulator.py:95`). When no pair is within this threshold:

- $n_c = 0$, $J_n = \emptyset$, $J_t = \emptyset$
- $D$ collapses to shape $(27, 0)$ (`control/lcs_formulator.py:275`: `D = np.zeros((n_x, 0))`)
- TOT = $n_x + 0 + n_u = 34$, total\_dim = $20 \times 34 + 27 = 707$ instead of 807

**Log evidence**: `results/week4_east_baseline.txt:32`:
```
[DEBUG] n_contacts=0, n_c=0, TOT=34, total_dim=707
[C3] step=1 n_c=0  |u[0]|=35.443 Nm
```

Without $D$, the QP objective has no way to push the box — only the EE-approach
Jacobian term (Section 5.2) provides a gradient, driving the arm toward the
pre-computed proxy.

---

### 9.2 Wrong-Face Local Minimum

If the contact normal satisfies $\hat{n}_{\text{onto\_box}} \cdot \hat{g} < 0$, then any
$\lambda_{n,i} > 0$ force pushes the box *away* from the goal. The QP's optimal
response is to set $\lambda_{n,i} = 0$ for all $i$ and find no useful trajectory.

This is the documented failure of local C3 on the WEST directional task: the arm
approaches from the east face (box is between arm and goal), the contact normal
points westward ($\hat{n} = [-1,0,0]$), and $\hat{n} \cdot \hat{g}_{\text{west}} = +1 > 0$
— but the force direction $\hat{n}_{\text{onto\_box}} = [+1,0,0]$ satisfies
$\hat{n}_{\text{onto\_box}} \cdot \hat{g}_{\text{west}} = -1 < 0$,
so every planned contact force pushes the box further east, away from the
$[-0.3, 0]$ goal.

Diagnostic (`control/ci_mpc_c3.py:246-251`): `[FORCE]` print shows `←WRONG ✗` when
`F·g_hat < 0`.

---

### 9.3 Polyhedral Friction-Cone Bias

The 4-edge polyhedral cone aligns its edges with $\hat{t}_1$ and $\hat{t}_2$ (Eq. 2.5),
which are determined by the contact normal via the cross-product construction.
For a perpendicular contact ($\hat{n} = \hat{y}$, pushing northward), the tangent
axes $\hat{t}_1, \hat{t}_2$ happen to include $\hat{x}$ — aligned with the east/west
goal directions. This gives those configurations more tangential-force capacity
"for free" in the QP than face-aligned contacts, causing the cost evaluator to
systematically under-price perpendicular contacts on directional pushes. This
was observed in the global-sampling cost-comparison experiment where the south-face
candidate (k=2, $\hat{n} = \hat{y}$) beat the west-face candidate (k=1, $\hat{n} = \hat{x}$)
by ≈15k cost units on the EAST task despite the west face being geometrically
closer to optimal.
